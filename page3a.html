Before looking at lenses or frames the first thing we need to do is look at your prescription. This will give all the clues you need to choose great glasses.  There are four parts to your prescription.

 

1. Spherical power (Sph).

Everybody has one. If the spherical power has a minus above the power, then this prescription will help you with your distance vision. If the spherical power has a plus sign above it, it will help magnify your vision.

 

Astigmatism

A spherical prescription means your eye will be rounder and the centre of the eye is in the centre of the eye. With an astigmatism your eye is more oval – at an extreme it is like a rugby ball. The centre of the of your eye can be located within a wide area. This means light is not directed to the centre of your eye.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

2. Cyl and Axis

The second part is for astigmatism. Before you ask – it is perfectly normal to have astigmatism. Everyone thinks their eyes are round and the centre of the eye is in the … well … centre of the eye. In an extreme situation this is true. Not when you have an astigmatism.

3. Near  and Intermediate Adds.

This is extra power needed to help you read without extending your arms or to see into the intermediate – looking at your computer screen for example. This is always a plus number.

 

4. Recall

This is when you should have your next sight test. It is also a use by date for the prescription.

 

Look at your prescription and ask yourself what my eyes need help with: Distance, intermediate, near or all of the above.

 

Not focusing light into the centre of  your eye can cause mild irritation to serious visual impairment depending of the size of your astigmatism. The Cyl refers to the extra power your prescription needs to help the astigmatism and the axis refers to the angle the light needs to be bent to reach the centre of your eye.  The cyl can be written as + or a -. This isn’t an assessment of your or your eye’s attitude during the sight test, but two different ways optometrists can write a prescription – don’t ask me why, probably to look smart.
